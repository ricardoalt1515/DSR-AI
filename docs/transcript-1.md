Okay, starting my site walkthrough now. I'm at the Southgate Industrial Campus main entrance, and I'll document each area in order with the waste streams I can verify on the ground. First stop is Building A, operated by Metropak Solutions. Building A is the packaging and printing plant, and it produces several streams every week. The biggest stream here is clean post-industrial plastic film trim from bag-making lines, mostly polyethylene and polypropylene. Current volume is about two tons per week. They bale it indoors near Dock A3 and sell it to a recycler in Portland. Pickup is twice per week. They also generate a second film stream that is lower quality, contaminated film with ink and adhesive residue. That one is around 500 pounds per week and currently goes to landfill because buyers reject contamination at this level. On the northeast side of Building A, I inspected the hazardous accumulation room. I counted twelve 55-gallon drums of spent solvents, mainly toluene and xylene from print cleanup. They are in secondary containment and labeled correctly. Generation rate is about three drums per month. Hauler is Heritage Environmental Services on a quarterly schedule. In the same room, there are around 200 spent fluorescent tubes boxed as universal waste. Also in a locked cage nearby, I saw expired adhesives and sealants, roughly 20 pallet equivalents, mixed condition, some unopened and some expired. Now moving to Building B, the metal fabrication shop run by North Forge Metals. Behind the building, there are two roll-off containers for ferrous scrap steel collected weekly by Schnitzer. There is one separate container for aluminum and copper cutoffs, filling roughly every 10 days at around 800 pounds per load. Inside the polishing area, they generate stainless steel fines and grinding dust, about 200 pounds per month. They currently dispose of it as potentially hazardous pending chromium testing. Next is the shared wastewater treatment zone for the campus. The packaged treatment system handles process water from multiple tenants. Sludge output is about four cubic yards per month of dewatered biosolids. Right now they landfill it. There is also oil and grease skimmings, around 50 gallons per month, sent to a rendering facility in Tacoma. Now I'm at Building C, operated by Cascade Frozen Foods. Their largest stream is organics from processing, fruit and vegetable trimmings, rejected product and expired inventory. Volume is high, around 8 tons per week, currently hauled to compost by Republic Services. They also produce cardboard and plastic film from packaging operations, roughly 2 tons per week combined, baled for recycling. Behind Building C is a tank farm. Two tanks hold used cooking oil from fryer lines, around 3,000 gallons per month, picked up by darling ingredients for biodiesel. Other tanks store cleaning chemicals and sanitizers. One sanitizer tank label is faded and should be corrected for compliance. Finally, in the open yard between Building C and the perimeter fence, I observed mixed materials with unclear ownership, broken concrete and masonry debris, maybe 20 pallet equivalents, plus a weathered pallet pile around 300 units. Some pallets are reusable, some are rotted and should be separated for wood waste processing. Ownership here is not clearly assigned, so this area may require mapping before final assignment. Summary by location. Building A has clean and contaminated plastic film plus solvents, lamps and expired adhesive inventory. Building B has ferrous and non-ferrous metals plus polishing dust. Shared wastewater area has biosolids and oil skimmings. Building C has organics, cardboard and film plus used cooking oil at the rear tank farm. The shared outdoor strip has unmanaged debris and pallets that need owner assignment.
